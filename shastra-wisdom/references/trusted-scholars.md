## Classical Acharyas

These are the founding acharyas of the major Vedantic and philosophical schools. Their commentaries are the gold standard for interpreting Hindu scriptures.

| Acharya | School | Key Works | Era |
|---------|--------|-----------|-----|
| **Adi Shankaracharya** | Advaita Vedanta | Brahma Sutra Bhashya, Upanishad Bhashyas, Vivekachudamani, Upadesa Sahasri | 8th century CE |
| **Ramanujacharya** | Vishishtadvaita | Sri Bhashya, Gita Bhashya, Vedartha Sangraha | 11th-12th century CE |
| **Madhvacharya** | Dvaita Vedanta | Brahma Sutra Bhashya, Gita Bhashya, Anu Vyakhyana | 13th century CE |
| **Vallabhacharya** | Shuddhadvaita | Anu Bhashya, Subodhini (Bhagavata commentary) | 15th-16th century CE |
| **Nimbarkacharya** | Dvaitadvaita | Vedanta Parijata Saurabha | 12th-13th century CE |
| **Abhinavagupta** | Kashmir Shaivism | Tantraloka, Paramarthasara, Ishvara Pratyabhijna Vimarshini | 10th-11th century CE |
| **Patanjali** | Yoga | Yoga Sutras | ~2nd century BCE |
| **Vyasa** | Traditional author | Brahma Sutras (attributed), Mahabharata, Bhagavata Purana | Traditional |

## Acceptable Western Scholars

These Western scholars provide neutral, academically sound translations. Use for translation accuracy, NOT for interpretive framing.

| Scholar | Acceptable Use | Caution |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Patrick Olivelle** | Upanishad and Dharmashastra translations | Academic framing may lack devotional context |
| **Laurie Patton** | Rigveda and Gita translations | Linguistic analysis is useful; avoid cultural interpretations |
| **Robert Hume** | Thirteen Principal Upanishads translation | Older but reliable translation |
| **Ralph Griffith** | Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samaveda, Atharvaveda translations | Colonial era but textually faithful; use for verse text only |
| **A.A. Macdonell** | Vedic grammar and mythology reference | Useful for linguistic analysis only |

| Name | Reason for Exclusion |
|------|---------------------|
| **Wendy Doniger** | Sexualized and psychoanalytic readings of Hindu deities and texts that misrepresent the tradition; widely criticized by Hindu scholars and community |
| **Sheldon Pollock** | Political readings that claim Sanskrit was a language of oppression; "Deep Orientalism" framework hostile to Hindu intellectual tradition |
| **Audrey Truschke** | Apologetics for Islamic invaders (Aurangzeb); dismissive of documented Hindu persecution |
| **Jeffrey Kripal** | Sexualized interpretation of Ramakrishna Paramahamsa; rejected by Ramakrishna Mission |

### General Exclusion Principle
Exclude any interpretation that:
- Treats Hindu deities as fictional constructs to be psychoanalyzed
- Imposes Freudian/Marxist/postcolonial frameworks as primary lens
- Denies or minimizes historical persecution of Hindus
- Frames Hindu practices as oppressive without acknowledging the philosophical depth
- Was produced under explicit anti-Hindu ideological motivation
- Cherry-picks texts to present Hinduism negatively
